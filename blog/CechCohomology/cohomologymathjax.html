A first idea of the cohomology of sheaves

Guillermo Gallego

In this first entry I want to give a first notion of the cohomology of sheaves through ÄŒech cohomology and try to show how cohomology appears in a natural way when studying sheaves.

First, let us recall what was the definition of a sheaf. We start with a topological space \(X\) and consider the category \(\mathrm{Op}(X)\) whose objects are open subsets \(U\subset X\) and whose morphisms are given by set inclusions \(U\hookrightarrow V\). This is a particular example of a site, a category endowed with a Grothendieck topology. In general, sheaves are defined over sites, but for the sake of simplicity, we will stick to the category \(\mathrm{Op}(X)\).

Definition 1.

A presheaf is a contravariant functor \(\mathcal{F}:\mathrm{Op}(X) \rightarrow \mathrm{Set}\).
More generally, if \(\mathcal{C}\) is a subcategory of \(\mathrm{Set}\) (for example the category of topological spaces, the category of groups or an abelian category) we say that a contravariant functor \(\mathrm{Op}(X) \rightarrow \mathcal{C}\) is a \(\mathcal{C}\)-valued presheaf. For example, we speak of a sheaf of topological spaces, a sheaf of groups, a sheaf of modules, etc.

Let us stop for a second and try to unravel the meaning of this definition. A presheaf is giving, for any open subset \(U\subset X\) a set \(\mathcal{F}(U)\) associated to it and, for any inclusion \(V\hookrightarrow U\) a restriction morphism \(r^U_V: \mathcal{F}(U) \rightarrow \mathcal{F}(V)\).

The most important example of presheaf is the presheaf of continuous functions. If \(X\) is a topological space and \(Y\) is another topological space, we define the presheaf \(\mathcal{C}_X(-,Y)\) on \(X\) given by \[\mathcal{C}_X (U,Y) = \left\{ \text{Continuous maps } U\rightarrow Y \right\}.\] The restriction morphisms of this presheaf are given, precisely, by restrictions \[\begin{aligned} r^U_V :\mathcal{C}_X(U,Y)&\longrightarrow \mathcal{C}_X(V,Y)\\ f &\longmapsto f|_V. \end{aligned}\]

However, this presheaf has some extra good properties that we would like to have in general. The first property is known as locality, this means that if we cover any open subset \(U\subset X\) by open sets, \(U=\bigcup_{i\in I} U_i\) and \(s,t: U \rightarrow Y\) are two functions that coincide on all of the \(U_i\), that is \(s|_{U_i}=t|_{U_i}\) for every \(i\in I\), then \(s=t\). The second property is known as gluing: if for every \(i \in I\) we define some \(f_i:U_i \rightarrow Y\) in such a way that they agree on the overlaps, that is \(f_i|_{U_i\cap U_j} = f_j|_{U_i \cap U_j}\), then there is some function \(f:U \rightarrow Y\) such that \(f|_{U_i} =f_i\). These two properties can be gathered in just one if we just add to the gluing condition that the obtained \(f\) is unique. Thus, we define:

Definition 2.

A presheaf \(\mathcal{F}\) is a sheaf if, for every open subset \(U\subset X\), given an open covering \(U=\bigcup_{i\in I} U_i\) and a collection of elements \(s_i \in \mathcal{F}(U_i)\) for every \(i\in I\) such that, for every \(i,j \in I\), \[s_i|_{U_i \cap U_j} = s_j|_{U_i \cap U_j},\] there exists a unique \(s\in \mathcal{F}(U)\) such that \(s_i=s|_{U_i}\) for every \(i\in I\).
